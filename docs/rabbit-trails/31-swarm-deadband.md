## A submarine captain who nearly died

I still remember the day that my submarine, the "Deep Explorer", was involved in a near-fatal accident. As the captain, I had been pushing my crew to optimize our route for speed, determined to break a new record for the fastest voyage across the ocean floor. My focus was on shaving off precious minutes, not on careful planning and attention to potential obstacles.

In retrospect, I realize that I had fallen into a trap, one that I now refer to as the "greedy optimization" trap. I was so fixated on achieving my goal that I neglected the most critical step in any successful journey: mapping out what NOT to do. This is what I call the "P0" or "Deadband Protocol". It's a crucial step that involves identifying potential hazards and obstacles, and taking steps to avoid them.

In our case, we failed to do this. We didn't take the time to thoroughly map out the seafloor, and as a result, we stumbled into a hidden underwater canyon. The submarine was badly damaged, and we were lucky to escape with our lives. It was a harrowing experience, and one that taught me a valuable lesson about the importance of careful planning and attention to detail.

Looking back, I realize that I had prioritized speed over safety, and that was a grave mistake. If I had taken the time to map out the obstacles and hazards, we could have avoided the accident altogether. This is what I now refer to as "P1" or creating a "safe space". By taking the time to identify potential risks, we can create a safe and stable environment that allows us to operate with confidence.

The final step, "P2" or optimization, is only possible once we have created a safe space. This is where we can focus on optimizing our route, our systems, and our processes to achieve our goals. But without a safe space, optimization is meaningless. It's like trying to build a house on quicksand - no matter how efficient the design, it will ultimately collapse.

My experience taught me that the "Deadband Protocol" is essential for success. By following the sequence of P0 (mapping out what NOT to do), P1 (creating a safe space), and P2 (optimizing), we can avoid the pitfalls of greedy optimization and achieve our goals safely and efficiently. The proof is in the numbers: when we prioritize speed over safety, the results are dismal (0/50), but when we follow the Deadband Protocol, the results are dramatically improved (50/50).

As I look back on that fateful day, I am reminded of the importance of humility and the need to learn from our mistakes. I am grateful for the lessons I learned, and I hope that my story can serve as a cautionary tale for others. Remember, optimization is important, but safety and careful planning must always come first.

It may seem counterintuitive, but trust me, it's a crucial part of ensuring a safe and successful climb.

So, why do I plan escape routes first? Well, it all comes down to risk management. When you're climbing a mountain, you're constantly assessing and mitigating risks. And one of the biggest risks is getting stuck on the mountain with no way down. By planning our escape routes first, we can identify potential hazards and obstacles, and make sure we have a safe way out in case things go wrong.

It's a bit like the old adage "plan for the worst, hope for the best." By mapping out our escape routes, we're essentially planning for the worst-case scenario. We're identifying the most likely routes down the mountain, taking into account factors like weather, terrain, and potential hazards. And by doing so, we can ensure that we have a safe and reliable way out, no matter what happens.

I remember one particular climb where we encountered a sudden snowstorm that reduced visibility to near zero. We were high up on the mountain, and the winds were howling. But because we had planned our escape route in advance, we were able to quickly and safely make our way down the mountain, avoiding some of the most treacherous terrain.

It's experiences like that which have taught me the importance of planning escape routes first. It's not just about getting to the summit; it's about getting back down safely, too. And by prioritizing our escape routes, we can ensure that we have a safe and successful climb, every time.

In fact, I like to think of it as a form of "inverted planning." Instead of focusing on the summit, we're focusing on the descent. We're thinking about how we're going to get back down the mountain, and what we need to do to stay safe. And by doing so, we can actually climb with more confidence and freedom, knowing that we have a solid plan in place.

So, the next time you're planning a climb, remember to plan your escape routes first. It may seem like a minor detail, but trust me, it's a crucial part of ensuring a safe and successful climb. And who knows, it might just save your life one day.

In the end, it's all about being prepared and staying safe on the mountain. And by planning our escape routes first, we can do just that. So, the next time you're planning a climb, take a page out of my book and plan your escape routes first. Your safety – and your life – may depend on it.
